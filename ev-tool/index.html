EV Calculator Info

The formula for this calculation is:
EV = log2(100*ƒ²/(ISO*time))
formula source
It hasn't yet been mathematically verified in accordance to the information detailed below, but it does seem to work in accordance with other online calculators.
EV = log2(ƒ²/t)
wikipedia
Correct Exposure Calculation ISO 2720:1974
(N2/t)=(LS/K)
Correction for ISO
EV100 = EVS-log2(S/100)

    Key:

  • N = Relative aperture (ƒ or f-number)
  • t = Exposure time (shutter speed) in seconds
  • L = Luminance (average of scene)
  • S = ISO number, sensitivity or 'speed'
  • K = Reflected-light meter calibration constant (ISO 2720:1974 recommends 10.6 to 13.4 cd/m2)

Neutral Density, or "ND" filters are darkened camera lens attachments that fit over the front of a lens to reduce the amount of light that enters a camera. This is handy if you want to use specific settings (such as a large aperture) that aren't suited to the environment or lighting conditions that you're shooting in. In a pinch, a fine mesh or stretched stocking can do the trick.
Polarising filters reduce reflected light, resulting in deeper color saturation and less reflections. Useful for shooting through windows or over water surfaces, they can sometimes drop a couple stops of exposure from a bright scene, but it's usually worth it. They work best at specific angles, so the amount of polarisation can vary across the scene when using wide angle lenses or shooting in indirect light (like at sunset).
Strobes are flashes. They throw out intense light and professional ones can usually be configured to trigger for a specific time for a specific light intensity. When using them, make sure your shutter and the strobe trigger at the same time or your camera won't capture the light from the strobe.
Hot Mirrors are essentially infrared filters which reflect longer wavelengths of light but allow shorter wavelengths to pass through them. Most modern digital cameras have hot mirrors to prevent IR from contaminating the image.
Cold Mirrors work inversely to cold mirrors, allowing you to capture infrared while reflecting away the Visible Light.
